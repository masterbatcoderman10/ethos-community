1. Core business model
Ethos Community™ should be positioned as a Community-as-a-Service platform serving displaced, diaspora and fragile-community markets. Kushian™ will be the Sudan-focused flagship pilot.
The business model should show that the platform can generate sustainable revenues through:
Platform/service fees on verified service transactions, where legally permissible.
Subscription fees from community associations, diaspora chapters and professional networks.
Referral or commission fees from approved service providers, where permissible and transparently disclosed.
Training, CPD and certification programme fees.
SME finance-readiness and advisory fees.
Administration fees for structured SME recovery support pools through regulated finance partners.
Future white-label licensing to NGOs, Islamic financial institutions, development agencies, community foundations and IsDB member-country partners.
The key message is that this is not a charity app. It is a scalable human-development infrastructure that converts informal diaspora support into verified, purpose-linked and measurable outcomes.
2. Technology and business partnerships
The prototype should show that Ethos Community™ does not operate alone. It connects multiple partner categories through a controlled digital ecosystem:
Technology partner: iWire, as web-app, mobile-app, dashboard and integration developer.
Payment and fintech partners: for regulated payment flows, wallets, cards or remittance-linked functionality, subject to licensing.
Healthcare and Takaful partners: clinics, hospitals, telemedicine providers, pharmacies, Takaful operators and cooperative insurance partners.
Education and CPD partners: universities, online learning providers, professional institutes and certification bodies.
Professional service partners: lawyers, accountants, auditors, tax advisers, immigration/documentation advisers and business consultants.
SME finance partners: Islamic finance institutions, banks, microfinance companies, fintech lenders and development finance partners.
Community partners: diaspora associations, women networks, student groups, professional associations, NGOs and community ambassadors.
The prototype should make this ecosystem visible, because partnerships are central to credibility, execution and scalability.
3. Key stakeholders
The platform should clearly show the main stakeholder groups:
Diaspora supporters, including working children, professionals, family members and community sponsors.
Beneficiaries, including parents, students, patients, women, widows, displaced professionals and SMEs.
Service providers, including healthcare providers, education providers, mentors and professional advisers.
Community ambassadors, who help verify needs, onboard users and support local trust-building.
Finance and Takaful partners, who provide regulated financial, health and risk-protection pathways.
Development partners, including IsDB Group entities, NGOs, foundations and public-sector stakeholders.
Platform operator, Ethos Community™, providing governance, digital workflows, partner onboarding, verification logic and impact reporting.
4. Target markets
The first pilot market is Sudanese displaced and diaspora communities under the Kushian™ brand.
The priority corridors should include:
Sudan and internal displacement locations.
Egypt, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar and Uganda as immediate host-market corridors.
UK, Europe and North America as diaspora-support corridors.
Later replication to other OIC communities, including Yemen, Palestine, Syria, Somalia, Iraq and Libya.
Muslim and diaspora communities outside IsDB member countries where family-support, education, health and SME-recovery needs are significant.
The key competition point is scalability. Kushian™ is the proof of concept, while Ethos Community™ is the replicable platform model.
5. Key service offering
Please reflect these seven core services in the prototype:
Verified community identity and beneficiary registration
Registration for diaspora supporters, beneficiaries, mentors, providers, SMEs and community ambassadors, including document upload and basic verification.
Purpose-linked family support
Family support linked to approved categories such as health, education, rent, legal documentation, emergency support, training and SME recovery.
Education, CPD and mentorship
Student-support cases, credential preservation, mentor matching, women CPD, employability training, digital skills and professional development.
Healthcare and Medical Takaful pathway
Verified healthcare cases, clinic referrals, telemedicine support, hospitalization pathways and referral to licensed Takaful or cooperative insurance partners.
Women empowerment and family resilience
Dedicated pathways for widows, female-headed households, women graduates, women professionals and women-led SMEs.
Professional services and legal support marketplace
Access to trusted legal, accounting, tax, audit, immigration, documentation, property, inheritance and compliance advisers.
SME recovery and finance-readiness support
Business diagnostics, licensing guidance, accounting setup, compliance checklist, cash-flow preparation, Islamic finance readiness and referral to regulated finance partners.
6. Suggested use cases to illustrate in the prototype
To make the MVP more practical and competition-ready, I suggest adding the following seven use cases:
Use Case 1: Diaspora doctor supporting elderly parents
A Sudanese doctor in the UK registers as a diaspora supporter, adds her elderly parents in Egypt as beneficiaries, selects “healthcare support”, uploads basic documents and receives a referral to a vetted clinic or telemedicine provider. The dashboard confirms service completion and records the impact.
Use Case 2: Banker in the UAE supporting university students in Cairo
A Sudanese banker in Dubai supports two siblings studying at Cairo University. He creates an education-support case, links payments to tuition and living costs, uploads student details and receives periodic academic progress updates.
Use Case 3: Legal adviser in Saudi Arabia helping parents displaced to Uganda
A Sudanese legal professional in Saudi Arabia uses the platform to connect his displaced parents in Uganda with local documentation, residency and legal-support advisers. This reduces informal exploitation and gives the family a verified support pathway.
Use Case 4: Widowed women and family resilience support in Sudan
A diaspora family member creates a family-resilience case for widowed sisters in North Sudan. A community ambassador verifies the case, links the family to living support, education support for children and women CPD or income-generation options.
Use Case 5: Woman dentist establishing a clinic in Sharjah
A Sudanese woman dentist in Sharjah seeks to establish a small clinic serving families and displaced professionals. The platform supports licensing guidance, clinic setup, business advisory, finance-readiness and potential onboarding into the healthcare provider network.
Use Case 6: Relocated SME owner needing working capital and compliance support
A Sudanese restaurant or grocery owner in Riyadh or Doha uploads a business profile, receives a compliance checklist, cash-flow template, accounting setup support and referral to an Islamic finance or microfinance partner.
Use Case 7: Sudanese product traders rebuilding export supply chains
Sudanese traders dealing in sesame, Arabic gum, karkadi or baobab use the platform for product profiling, export-readiness checks, buyer introductions, documentation support and finance-readiness referrals. This protects livelihoods and preserves Sudanese trade corridors.

8. Message to emphasize in the demo
The core message should be:
Kushian™ is the Sudan pilot. Ethos Community™ is the scalable platform infrastructure. The model can be adapted across OIC member-country communities affected by displacement, migration, fragile economies, disrupted education, weak healthcare access and SME displacement.
This scalability, combined with measurable human-development impact, should be central to the IsDB competition positioning.
